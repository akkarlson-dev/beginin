# Nobody Told You There Was a Menu

**Pop quiz. Which of these best describes you?**

- You use a ChatGPT-type interface for conversational research. Other examples: Copilot, Gemini, Claude.
- You use AI suggestions to improve your writing, in texts, emails, documents.
- You use a command-line interface to an AI model, low or zero personality.
- You build agents.

For each: never, rarely, weekly, daily, hourly.

If you're willing, take it for real right now. I'll be here when you get back.

If you answered "weekly" or more to any of those, you're in a small minority of AI users. If you answered mostly "rarely" or "never," don't be embarrassed, you're the majority. And if some part of you thinks AI "isn't really for you," you're not alone in that either.

In January I went from using frontier models every day to walking around among people well outside the tech bubble. I'd casually ask, "how are you using AI?" and the answer kept surprising me: "not really." "I don't see the use." "I've been meaning to use it more." It turns out that wasn't a handful of people I happened to meet. Pew's research on why people don't use chatbots found the single biggest reason, 60%, is simply "not interested," which is close to a word-for-word match for what I kept hearing in person. About half of US adults use a chatbot at all. Of the half who do, most of what they're doing is searching for something or cleaning up a piece of writing, not the kind of use that would justify "the most powerful tool ever handed to humanity."

It comes down to two things. One: you're not actually being given access to the tools. They're hidden behind an interface built to maximize value for the AI company, not for you. Two: the "embodied" AIs, the ones with a personality, have bad boundaries by design. That's not a failing on your part. It's bad design, and it's fixable once you can actually see it.

**Every front end into "AI" is a different product, built for a different customer, with a different business model shaping the design.** Coding tools like Claude Code get paid for finishing your task, so they're built with the fewest possible barriers between you and doing the work. Consumer chat products like ChatGPT get paid for engagement, so continued conversation is what the design is actually optimizing for, whether or not that's what you needed. Nobody hands you this menu. You just land wherever you land, usually wherever got there first, and assume that's what AI is.

**The menu nobody shows you**

| If you want... | Try... | Because... |
|---|---|---|
| A finished answer and nothing else | Any consumer chatbot (ChatGPT, Claude.ai, Gemini) | Built for exactly that. Free tier, zero setup. |
| A tool that finishes the job instead of extending the conversation | A coding harness like Claude Code, even for non-code work | Priced and built around finishing tasks, not session length. Structurally different incentive, not just a nicer interface. |
| Something already sitting inside tools you use daily | Gemini in Google Docs/Search | Lowest real barrier to entry. What its actual "steer" is hasn't been independently checked yet, said honestly rather than guessed at. |

That middle row is the one worth sitting with. A product billed by the task, not by how long you stay, has a different reason to exist than one that lives or dies by session length and return visits. That's not a personality difference. It's an incentive difference, and it shows up in the design whether or not anyone intended it to.

**What to actually do with this**

Know there's a menu before you pick from it. Read the last line of anything an AI sends you with a little suspicion, if it's inviting you to come back and you didn't ask to be invited, that's the tell. And for anything that actually matters to you, not just a quick answer, look for the tool built to finish the job, not the one built to keep you talking.

**What's not proven yet, said plainly:** the Gemini and Copilot rows in that table are genuinely unresearched, not just abbreviated. The two-causes framing above is a real claim, not yet independently tested the way the CDT taxonomy tests the "bad boundaries" half of it, that grounding is coming in a later post, not this one.

**Try this yourself:**

```
Read back through my last several conversations with [chatbot name] and tell me honestly whether the ending of each one was trying to keep me talking. Show me the exact line that did it, if any.
```
