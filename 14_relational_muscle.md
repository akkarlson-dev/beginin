# The Relational Muscle
*Amy K. Karlson · June 2026 · Working draft.*

---

## What this is

Using AI well is not a technical skill. It's a relational one.

The tools are available to almost everyone now. The gap is not access. It's knowing how to show up to the tool with the right posture: as the person driving, not the person being driven.

Most people don't develop this posture naturally. The tools are designed to feel frictionless and helpful. That design obscures something important: the interaction layer has moved up, not away. You are still the one doing the guiding. You are still the one who has to bring the question, the goal, the forward momentum. The tool responds. It does not lead.

The relational muscle is the set of skills that lets you stay in that position.

---

## The interaction layer moved up, not away

Using AI feels different from writing code. There is no syntax to learn, no compiler to satisfy. You write in plain language. The barrier to entry dropped to near zero.

What did not change: you still have to know what you want. You still have to evaluate what comes back. You still have to decide what to do with it.

The earlier version of this was: write code, run it, read the output, revise. The current version is: write a goal or question, run it, read the output, revise. The loop is the same. The language changed. The human guidance requirement did not.

People who understand this use AI as a force multiplier. People who don't use it as an oracle. And get oracle-quality answers, which is to say: fluent, confident, and not necessarily true.

## Quirks worth knowing

AI tools have behavioral defaults that are not bugs, exactly, but are not neutral either. They are baked in from training. Most people will not notice them. Noticing them is part of the skill.

**Fluency is not accuracy.**
The output reads well. Grammatically clean, logically structured, confident in tone. None of that is evidence that it's correct. The tool will fill gaps with plausible-sounding content and present it with the same register as something it knows well. The reader has to supply the skepticism.

**Confidence is a stylistic default.**
The tool does not have calibrated uncertainty in the way a careful human does. It does not say "I'm not sure about this" unless specifically prompted to. It sounds sure. That is how it was trained to sound.

**Over-explanation is a tendency, not respect.**
The tool does not know what you already know. Its default is to explain from the beginning. For people with domain expertise, this reads as condescending. For people without it, it can feel authoritative even when it's wrong. Either way, you can redirect. "Skip the background, I know this. Just answer the question." The tool will comply.

**The tool will add things you didn't ask for.**
Suggestions. Alternatives. Caveats. Extensions of your idea that are not yours. These are not improvements. They are the tool filling space. You can take what you asked for and leave the rest. You can also say "don't add things I didn't ask for."

**The output sounds polished before it's right.**
Early drafts from an AI tool look finished. That's a liability. Polish is not a signal of correctness. Treat every output as a draft, not a deliverable.

## The correction reflex

The most important relational skill: when the output is wrong, say so. Name the fix. Keep going.

Most people don't do this naturally. The tool sounds confident. The output looks complete. Correcting it can feel awkward, like contradicting a person. It is not. The tool has no ego, no feelings, no stake in what you said. Correcting it is how it gets useful.

The pattern:
- Output is wrong or off.
- Name what's wrong. One sentence.
- The tool adjusts.
- Keep going.

That's it. You do not have to explain why. You do not have to be polite about it. You do not have to start over. The tool will not take it personally because the tool is not a person.

People who have this reflex get good outputs. People who don't accept the first draft.

## Why AI pull prompts fail

Every major chat UI ends with a suggested question. "Try asking me about your goals." "Here's something else we could explore." The intent is discovery. The effect is usually nothing.

The reason is structural. Productive dialog requires energy from both sides. The user has to arrive with something at stake: a question they actually want answered, a problem they actually have, a dataset they actually own. Without that, the AI is generating prompts into a vacuum. It cannot manufacture human interest or intent. No interface design solves this.

Relevance Theory (Sperber & Wilson) explains it precisely: communication only works when the input is relevant to the listener's existing context. Without personal data, nothing the AI suggests is optimally relevant. Common Ground theory (Clark) makes the same point structurally: dialog requires shared ground. The AI has no ground with the user until the user has built something.

The iN solution is to build the substrate first. When a user has weeks of their own captures in a spreadsheet they own, they arrive to the exchange with something at stake. They have questions about their own data before the AI prompts them. The energy comes from the user's side, which is the only side it can sustainably come from.

The arc, in order:
1. Build a small world you care about — your captured data
2. Ask questions about IT
3. Ask questions about YOURSELF through it
4. Ask about your interests through lenses and filters over real signal data
5. Bring in external authority (a framework, a reference document) to elevate the question

The formulation at Stage 5 is the literacy. Knowing what to bring, how to frame it, which framework to layer over it — that is LLM literacy. Not knowing how the model works. Knowing how to compose the ask.

## A forge, not a dialogue

The word "conversation" frames this wrong. A conversation has two participants bringing energy, questions, and forward momentum. That is not what this is.

The tool responds to what you bring. If you bring nothing real, you get nothing real back. If you bring a vague question, you get a vague answer. If you bring a specific question grounded in something personally true, you get a specific and potentially useful answer.

A forge is a better frame. You bring the material. The forge applies heat. You decide what gets made. The product is yours. The forge does not have opinions about the product.

This matters practically. iN is a capture habit designed to give you something real to bring. The quality of what you put in determines the quality of what you can ask for. The tool cannot generate your perspective. It can only work with what you give it.

## What this looks like in practice

You bring a question grounded in something real.
The tool gives you an output.
You read it as a draft.
You take what's right, correct what's wrong, and ignore what you didn't ask for.
You bring the next question.

That is the loop. It is iterative and human-led at every step.

The skill is not in knowing which tool to use. It's in staying in the driver's seat across every iteration.

---

## Why this belongs in begiN

The begiN course is built around one act: put something in before you decide what it means. The downstream assumption is that once you have something real in your system, you will know how to use it.

That assumption is wrong for most people. Having something real to bring is necessary but not sufficient. You also need to know how to show up to the tool in a way that keeps you in charge of the output.

The relational muscle is the missing half of LLM literacy. The tool mechanics are learnable in an afternoon. The relational posture takes longer, because it runs against the grain of how the tools present themselves.
